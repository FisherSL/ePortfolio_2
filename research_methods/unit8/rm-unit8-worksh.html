Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 8 Heather

Heather Species Prevalence Analysis for Locations A and B

Hypothesis Testing

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no significant difference in the prevalence of the target species between Location A and Location B.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a significant difference in the prevalence of the target species between Location A and Location B.
  • Level of Significance (α): 0.05

Chi-Square Test for Independence - Step-by-Step Guide

Here is how I performed a Chi-square test for independence in Excel using the given data.

Step 1: Input the Data

First, I entered the data into an Excel worksheet in a format similar to the table below:

Prevalence Location A Location B
Absent 8 20
Sparse 22 14
Abundant 26 10
Total 56 44

Step 2: Calculate the Row Totals

Next, I calculated the row totals for each prevalence category:

  • In cell D2, I entered the formula =B2+C2 and dragged it down to D4.
  • In cell D5, I entered the formula =SUM(D2:D4).
Prevalence Location A Location B Row Totals
Absent 8 20 =B2+C2
Sparse 22 14 =B3+C3
Abundant 26 10 =B4+C4
Total 56 44 =SUM(D2:D4)

Step 3: Calculate the Expected Frequencies

Then, I calculated the expected frequency for each cell using the formula:

Eij = (Row Total × Column Total) / Grand Total

  • I created a new table for expected frequencies below the observed frequencies table.
  • In cell B8, I entered the formula =(D2*$B$5)/$D$5 and dragged it down to B10.
  • In cell C8, I entered the formula =(D2*$C$5)/$D$5 and dragged it down to C10.

Step 4: Find the Critical Value and Compare

  1. Calculate the expected frequencies (E) using the formula: 
    Eij = (Row Total * Column Total) / Grand Total
  2. Calculate the expected frequencies for each cell:
    • Absent, Location A: (56 * 28) / 100 = 15.68
    • Absent, Location B: (44 * 28) / 100 = 12.32
    • Sparse, Location A: (56 * 22) / 100 = 12.32
    • Sparse, Location B: (44 * 22) / 100 = 9.68
    • Abundant, Location A: (56 * 26) / 100 = 14.56
    • Abundant, Location B: (44 * 26) / 100 = 11.44
  3. Input the expected frequencies into another table in Excel.
  4. Use the formula for each cell to calculate the Chi-square component: 
    (Oi - Ei)^2 / Ei
  5. Calculate the Chi-square components for each cell:
    • Absent, Location A: (8 - 15.68)^2 / 15.68 ≈ 3.76
    • Absent, Location B: (20 - 12.32)^2 / 12.32 ≈ 4.77
    • Sparse, Location A: (22 - 12.32)^2 / 12.32 ≈ 7.63
    • Sparse, Location B: (14 - 9.68)^2 / 9.68 ≈ 1.93
    • Abundant, Location A: (26 - 27.44)^2 / 27.44 ≈ 0.07
    • Abundant, Location B: (10 - 11.56)^2 / 11.56 ≈ 0.21
  6. Sum these values to get the total Chi-square statistic: 
    χ² = 3.76 + 4.77 + 7.63 + 1.93 + 0.07 + 0.21 = 18.37
  7. Compare the calculated Chi-square value (18.37) to the critical value from the Chi-square distribution table with 2 degrees of freedom at a 0.05 significance level (which is approximately 5.991).
  8. Since 18.37 > 5.991, we reject the null hypothesis.

Conclusion

Given that the calculated Chi-square value (18.37) is greater than the critical value (5.991), we reject the null hypothesis. This indicates that there is a significant difference in the prevalence of the species between the two locations.

Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 8 Sample Statistics for Diet A and Diet B (Weight Loss)

The data relates to the weight losses achieved by two separate samples of 50 human subjects, each of whom undertook one of two different weight-reducing diets (A or B). The data is shown below:

Diet Wtloss
A 3.709
A 7.087
A 6.754
... ...
B 2.594
... ...

Step-by-Step Guide

1. State the Hypotheses

I start by stating the null and alternative hypotheses:

Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no difference in the mean weight loss between Diet A and Diet B.

Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a difference in the mean weight loss between Diet A and Diet B.

2. Set the Criteria for the Decision

I set the level of significance for the test. For this example, I choose a 5% level of significance (α = 0.05).

3. Compute the Test Statistic

To compute the test statistic, I follow these steps:

  1. Calculate the mean weight loss for each diet:
    2. Mean_A = (Sum of all weight losses for Diet A) / n_A
            Mean_B = (Sum of all weight losses for Diet B) / n_B
  2. Calculate the standard deviation for each diet:
    3. SD_A = sqrt(Sum of squared deviations from the mean for Diet A / (n_A - 1))
            SD_B = sqrt(Sum of squared deviations from the mean for Diet B / (n_B - 1))
  3. Calculate the standard error of the difference between the two means:
    4. SE_diff = sqrt((SD_A^2 / n_A) + (SD_B^2 / n_B))
  4. Compute the t-statistic:
    5. t = (Mean_A - Mean_B) / SE_diff

4. Make a Decision

Using the calculated t-statistic and the degrees of freedom, I compare the t-statistic to the critical t-value from the t-distribution table at the chosen significance level.

Degrees of freedom (df) can be approximated using the formula for unequal variances:

df = ((SD_A^2 / n_A) + (SD_B^2 / n_B))^2 / [((SD_A^2 / n_A)^2 / (n_A - 1)) + ((SD_B^2 / n_B)^2 / (n_B - 1))]

If the absolute value of the t-statistic is greater than the critical t-value, I reject the null hypothesis. Otherwise, I fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Conclusion

Given the data, I calculate the following:

  • Mean weight loss for Diet A: 5.341 kg
  • Standard deviation for Diet A: 2.536 kg
  • Mean weight loss for Diet B: 3.710 kg
  • Standard deviation for Diet B: 2.769 kg

Using these values, I compute the test statistic:

SE_diff = sqrt((2.536^2 / 50) + (2.769^2 / 50)) ≈ 0.523
            t = (5.341 - 3.710) / 0.523 ≈ 3.118

With 98 degrees of freedom (using the approximation formula for df), the critical t-value at α = 0.05 is approximately 1.984.

Since 3.118 > 1.984, I reject the null hypothesis.

Conclusion: There is a significant difference in the mean weight loss between Diet A and Diet B.

Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 8 Hypothesis Testing for Income Data

The data set shows the incomes of 60 male and 60 female cardholders of a certain bank's "Superplus Diamond" card:

Sex Income (£'000's)
... ...
M 40.6
M 54.6
M 38.6
... ...
F 57.8

Step-by-Step Guide

1. State the Hypotheses

I start by stating the null and alternative hypotheses:

Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no difference in the mean income between males and females.

Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a difference in the mean income between males and females.

2. Set the Criteria for the Decision

I set the level of significance for the test. For this example, I choose a 5% level of significance (α = 0.05).

3. Compute the Test Statistic

To compute the test statistic, I follow these steps:

  1. Calculate the mean income for males and females:
    2. Mean_Male = (Sum of all incomes for males) / n_male = 53.5183
            Mean_Female = (Sum of all incomes for females) / n_female = 42.3733
  2. Calculate the standard deviation for males and females:
    3. SD_Male = sqrt(Sum of squared deviations from the mean for males / (n_male - 1)) ≈ 15.0785
            SD_Female = sqrt(Sum of squared deviations from the mean for females / (n_female - 1)) ≈ 15.0396
  3. Calculate the pooled standard deviation:
    4. Pooled_SD = sqrt(((n_male - 1) * SD_Male^2 + (n_female - 1) * SD_Female^2) / (n_male + n_female - 2))
            Pooled_SD ≈ 15.0591
  4. Compute the t-statistic:
    5. t = (Mean_Male - Mean_Female) / (Pooled_SD * sqrt(1/n_male + 1/n_female))
            t ≈ 4.1925

4. Make a Decision

Using the calculated t-statistic and the degrees of freedom, I compare the t-statistic to the critical t-value from the t-distribution table at the chosen significance level.

Degrees of freedom (df) is equal to the sum of the sample sizes minus 2:

df = n_male + n_female - 2 = 118

Critical t-value for df = 118 at α = 0.05 (two-tailed) ≈ ±1.980.

Since the absolute value of the t-statistic (4.1925) is greater than the critical t-value (1.980), I reject the null hypothesis.

Conclusion

Conclusion: There is a significant difference in the mean income between males and females.

Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 8 Brand Preference Analysis Two-Sample t-Test

Step-by-Step Guide

1. State the Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no difference in mean brand preference between the two demographic areas.

Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a difference in mean brand preference between the two demographic areas.

2. Set the Criteria for the Decision

For this test, we choose a 5% level of significance (α = 0.05).

3. Compute the Test Statistic

Calculation of Test Statistic
Area 1 Area 2
Mean 2.3 2.0
Standard Deviation 0.71 0.63
Pooled Standard Deviation 0.68
t-Statistic 3.12

Calculation Details:

Calculation of Test Statistic
Area 1 Area 2
Mean (Brand A) =(SUMIF(A:A, 1, B:B) / COUNTIF(A:A, 1)) =(SUMIF(A:A, 2, B:B) / COUNTIF(A:A, 2))
Mean (Brand B) =(SUMIF(A:A, 1, B:B) / COUNTIF(A:A, 1)) =(SUMIF(A:A, 2, B:B) / COUNTIF(A:A, 2))
Mean (Other Brands) =(SUMIF(A:A, 1, B:B, "<>A") / COUNTIF(A:A, 1)) =(SUMIF(A:A, 2, B:B, "<>A") / COUNTIF(A:A, 2))

Calculation Details:

  • Mean (Brand A): Average brand preference score for Brand A in each area. Calculated by summing brand preference scores for Brand A in each area and dividing by the count of responses in Area 1 and Area 2, respectively.
  • Mean (Brand B): Average brand preference score for Brand B in each area. Calculated by summing brand preference scores for Brand B in each area and dividing by the count of responses in Area 1 and Area 2, respectively.
  • Mean (Other Brands): Average brand preference score for other brands in each area. Calculated by summing brand preference scores for all other brands (excluding Brand A and Brand B) in each area and dividing by the count of responses in Area 1 and Area 2, respectively.
  • Mean: Sum of brand preferences divided by the number of observations in each area.
  • Standard Deviation: Measure of dispersion of brand preferences within each area.
  • Pooled Standard Deviation: Combined measure of variability between the two areas.
  • t-Statistic: Measure of difference in mean brand preferences between the two areas.

4. Make a Decision

Using the calculated t-statistic (3.12) and degrees of freedom (48), we compare it to the critical t-value from the t-distribution table at the chosen significance level (α = 0.05).

For df = 48 and α = 0.05, the critical t-value is approximately ±2.010.

Since the absolute value of the calculated t-statistic (3.12) exceeds the critical t-value (2.010), we reject the null hypothesis.

Conclusion

Conclusion: Based on the two-sample t-test analysis, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a significant difference in mean brand preference between individuals in the two demographic areas.
