Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 7.2 Income Comparison

I conducted an analysis to determine whether the population mean income for males exceeds that of females using the provided bank cardholder data. Here's the detailed process I followed:

Formulating the Hypotheses

I started by formulating the hypotheses:

  • Null hypothesis (H0): μM ≤ μF (The mean income for males is less than or equal to the mean income for females)
  • Alternative hypothesis (HA): μM > μF (The mean income for males is greater than the mean income for females)

Assumptions

I made the following assumptions for the t-test:

  • The incomes for both males and females are normally distributed.
  • The variances of the two populations are equal (homogeneity of variance).
  • The samples are independent.

Conducting the Test

Next, I performed an independent two-sample t-test assuming equal variances. Here’s how I did it:

1. Calculating Descriptive Statistics

I calculated the mean and standard deviation for both male and female incomes using the following Excel formulas:

  • Mean for Males: =AVERAGE(B2:B61)
  • Mean for Females: =AVERAGE(B62:B121)
  • Standard Deviation for Males: =STDEV.P(B2:B61)
  • Standard Deviation for Females: =STDEV.P(B62:B121)

2. Using Excel's Data Analysis Toolpak

I enabled the Data Analysis Toolpak in Excel and followed these steps:

  • Went to Data -> Data Analysis -> t-Test: Two-Sample Assuming Equal Variances.
  • Selected the ranges for male (B2:B61) and female (B62:B121) income data.
  • Set the hypothesised mean difference to 0.
  • Chose a confidence level of 95%.
  • Selected the output range for the results.

3. Interpreting the Output

Based on the results from Excel:

Income M Income F
Mean 52.91 44.23
Variance 233.13 190.18

Difference in means 8.68

Conclusion

Based on the results:

  • The mean income for males is 8.68 units higher than the mean income for females.
  • The t-statistic is 3.27, indicating the number of standard errors that the observed difference in sample means is away from the hypothesised difference in population means.
  • The one-tailed p-value is 0.00071, which is less than the significance level of 0.05.
  • The t-statistic (3.27) is greater than the critical t-value for the one-tailed test (1.66), leading me to reject the null hypothesis.
Given that I reject the null hypothesis, I conclude that there is significant evidence to suggest that the mean income for males is greater than the mean income for females.

Assumptions Validation

To ensure the validity of this test, I would checked the following assumptions:

  • Normality: I would use normality tests like the Shapiro-Wilk test or Q-Q plots to check if the impurity distributions are approximately normal.
  • Equality of Variances: I would use Levene's test or an F-test to assess the homogeneity of variances.
  • Independence: The samples should be independent of each other.
By validating these assumptions, I can be confident in the results of my t-test analysis.

Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 7.3 Two-Tailed Test for Filtration Data

I conducted an analysis to determine whether the population mean impurity differs between the two filtration agents using the provided data set. Here's the detailed process I followed:

Formulating the Hypotheses

I started by formulating the hypotheses:

  • Null hypothesis (H0): μ1 = μ2 (The population mean impurity for Agent 1 is equal to that of Agent 2)
  • Alternative hypothesis (H1): μ1 ≠ μ2 (The population mean impurity for Agent 1 is not equal to that of Agent 2)

Assumptions

I made the following assumptions for the t-test:

  • The impurity measurements for both filtration agents are normally distributed.
  • The variances of the two populations are equal (homogeneity of variance).
  • The samples are independent.

Conducting the Test

Next, I performed a two-tailed t-test to compare the population mean impurity between the two filtration agents.

1. Calculating Descriptive Statistics

I calculated the mean and standard deviation for both filtration agents' impurity measurements using the following Excel formulas:

  • Mean for Agent 1: =AVERAGE(B2:B13)
  • Mean for Agent 2: =AVERAGE(C2:C13)
  • Standard Deviation for Agent 1: =STDEV.S(B2:B13)
  • Standard Deviation for Agent 2: =STDEV.S(C2:C13)

2. Performing the t-Test

I used Excel's Data Analysis Toolpak to perform the t-test:

  • Enabled the Data Analysis Toolpak in Excel.
  • Went to Data -> Data Analysis -> t-Test: Two-Sample Assuming Equal Variances.
  • Selected the impurity measurements for both filtration agents as input ranges.
  • Set the hypothesised mean difference to 0.
  • Chose a confidence level.
  • Selected the output range for the results.

Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 7.4 One-Tailed Test Comparison

For a one-tailed test to determine whether Filter Agent 1 is more effective, I would compare the mean impurity of Agent 1 to that of Agent 2 and focus on the direction of the difference rather than its magnitude.

Here's how the comparison would go based on the provided data:

  • Mean Impurity for Agent 1: 8.25
  • Mean Impurity for Agent 2: 8.683333333

Considering the means, it appears that Agent 1 has a lower impurity on average compared to Agent 2. However, I need to confirm if this difference is statistically significant in the context of a one-tailed test.

Given that the one-tailed t-test focuses on whether one mean is significantly greater or less than the other, I would analyse the t-statistic and p-value from the two-tailed test to infer this.

From the two-tailed test results:

  • t Stat: -1.026865443
  • P(T<=t) two-tail: 0.315642517
  • t Critical two-tail: 2.073873068

The t-statistic is negative, indicating that Agent 1's mean impurity is lower than Agent 2's mean impurity. However, the p-value is greater than the significance level of 0.05, suggesting that this difference is not statistically significant.

If I had conducted a one-tailed test focusing on whether Agent 1's impurity is less than Agent 2's, I would use the same t-statistic but halve the p-value from the two-tailed test (since the one-tailed test only looks at one direction of the distribution). If the halved p-value is less than 0.05, I would reject the null hypothesis and conclude that Agent 1 is indeed more effective than Agent 2 in reducing impurity.

However, since the p-value from the two-tailed test is already greater than 0.05, it's highly unlikely that halving it for the one-tailed test would yield a significant result. Therefore, based on the available data, I would still fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is insufficient evidence to suggest that Agent 1 is more effective than Agent 2 in reducing impurity.

Summary Measures Worksheet: Exercise 7.5 Analysis of Bank Cardholder Data

Assuming the data to be suitably distributed, complete an appropriate test of whether the population mean income for males exceeds that of females and interpret your findings. What assumptions underpin the validity of your analysis, and how could you validate them?

Step-by-Step Method:

  1. Formulate Hypotheses: Null hypothesis (H0): μM = μF (The population mean income for males is equal to that of females). Alternative hypothesis (H1): μM > μF (The population mean income for males exceeds that of females).
  2. Assumptions: We assume that the incomes for males and females are normally distributed, the variances of the two populations are equal, and the samples are independent.
  3. Collect Data: Collect the income data for males and females from Data Set C.
  4. Calculate Descriptive Statistics: Calculate the mean income for males (μM) and females (μF), and the standard deviations for both groups.
  5. Perform t-Test: Conduct an independent samples t-test to compare the mean incomes of males and females.
  6. Interpret Results: Based on the t-test results, determine whether there is a statistically significant difference between the mean incomes of males and females. If the p-value is less than the significance level (e.g., 0.05), reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the mean income for males exceeds that of females.
  7. Validate Assumptions: Validate the assumptions of normality, equality of variances, and independence using appropriate statistical tests or graphical methods (e.g., Shapiro-Wilk test for normality, Levene's test for equality of variances).
