Writing a Literature Review

Guiding questions to support planning, revising, and refining of a literature review*

  1. What is the focus and aim of your review? Who is your audience?
  2. Why is there a need for your review? Why is it significant?
  3. What is the context of the topic or issue? What perspective do you take? What framework do you use to synthesise the literature?
  4. How did you locate and select sources for inclusion in the review?
  5. How is your review structured?
  6. What are the main findings in the literature on this topic?
  7. What are the main strengths and limitations of this literature?
  8. Are there any discrepancies in this literature?
  9. What conclusions do your draw from the review? What do you argue needs to be done as an outcome of the review?

* As with other sets of guiding questions in this book, select those questions that are relevant to your context, add others as appropriate, and decide the order in which you will address them to communicate effectively with your audience.

Cloud security risks in a sector of your choice

"Cloud security risks in call tracking and CRM in MarTech"

  1. Focus and Aim: The focus of the literature review will be to explore the cloud security risks specific to the call tracking and martech sector, particularly within the context of an EU country. The aim is to assess the implications of third-party data transfers and global data housing on cloud security within this industry. The primary audience includes stakeholders involved in cloud security, data protection, call tracking, martech, and regulatory compliance.
  2. Need and Significance: The review is necessary due to the increasing reliance on cloud technologies in the call tracking and martech sector, coupled with the regulatory requirements imposed by the GDPR in EU countries. Understanding and mitigating cloud security risks are crucial for safeguarding sensitive data and maintaining compliance with data protection regulations. Additionally, the global nature of data housing and third-party data transfers adds complexity to cloud security practices, making this review significant for industry practitioners, policymakers, and researchers.
  3. Context and Perspective: The topic of cloud security risks in call tracking and martech is situated within the broader context of cybersecurity, data protection regulations, and technological advancements. The review will adopt a practical perspective, analysis of relevant frameworks, regulations, and case studies to assess the effectiveness of current security measures and identify areas for improvement.
  4. Source Selection: Sources for inclusion in the review will be identified through systematic searches of academic databases, industry reports, case studies, regulatory documents, and relevant literature on cloud security, data protection, call tracking, and martech. Selection criteria included relevance to the topic, methodological rigour, and geographical applicability to EU countries.

  1. Structure: The review will be structured into sections that address key aspects of cloud security risks in the call tracking and martech sector. These sections may include an overview of cloud security principles, an analysis of regulatory requirements (such as the GDPR), an examination of third-party data transfer mechanisms, an assessment of global data housing practices, case studies illustrating security incidents or best practices, and recommendations for mitigating risks.
  2. Main Findings: The literature on cloud security risks in the call tracking and martech sector include the identification of vulnerabilities associated with third-party data transfers, some mention of 3rd party data management with APIs, challenges in ensuring compliance with GDPR requirements, risks posed by global data housing practices, and the need for enhanced security measures to protect against data breaches and regulatory penalties. The layout would be surrounding these ideas:
    1. Vulnerabilities Associated with Cloud Data Storage:
      1. A primary focus of the literature is on vulnerabilities associated with cloud data storage in martech environments.
      2. Vulnerabilities in cloud data storage are a key focus.
      3. Issues include misconfigured storage, inadequate access controls, and encryption lapses.
      4. Consequences include unauthorised access and data leakage.
      5. Emphasises the need for robust security measures.
    2. Challenges in Securing Cloud-Based Martech Platforms: The literature discusses challenges in securing martech platforms. Focus includes marketing automation systems, CRM platforms, and data analytics tools.
      1. Key concerns involve data privacy, regulatory compliance, and customer data integrity.
      2. The dynamic nature of martech ecosystems complicates security implementation.
      3. Interconnected cloud services and third-party integrations add complexity.
    3. Risks of Data Residency and Jurisdictional Compliance: Global data residency requirements and jurisdictional differences in data protection laws pose significant risks for organisations utilising cloud infrastructure in martech. Challenges in ensuring compliance with diverse regulatory frameworks are examined.
      1. Concerns about navigating data sovereignty issues.
      2. Addressing challenges related to cross-border data transfers will be discussed.
      3. Strategies for managing data residency risks outlined.
      4. Measures include implementing data localisation practices.
      5. Compliance is emphasised.
      6. Contractual safeguards recommended to protect data sovereignty and regulatory compliance.
    4. Need for Enhanced Security Practices and Governance Frameworks: Need for enhanced security practices and governance frameworks. Implement robust access controls, encryption mechanisms, and data loss prevention (DLP) solutions.
      1. Establish comprehensive security policies to address martech security concerns.
      2. Conduct regular security assessments and audits to identify vulnerabilities.
      3. Emerging threats, evolving threats.
      4. Provide ongoing training and awareness programs to employees.
      5. Promote a culture of security across the martech ecosystem.
  3. Strengths and Limitations: The likely strengths of the literature will include comprehensive coverage of cloud security risks, detailed analyses of regulatory frameworks, and practical insights from industry case studies. However, limitations include gaps in research on emerging threats, inconsistencies in regulatory interpretations, and the evolving nature of cloud technologies and martech itself, which may outpace current security practices. CRMs are at the forefront, and this will influence future commerce as our digital landscape explodes with IoT and AI as part of the internet 4.0.
  4. Discrepancies:
    • Effectiveness of existing security measures in addressing vulnerabilities specific to CRM data stored in the cloud.
    • Challenges in ensuring GDPR compliance and mitigating risks associated with third-party data transfer via APIs.
  5. Conclusions and Recommendations:
    • Summary of insights into specific security vulnerabilities in CRM data stored in the cloud.
    • Recommendations for enhancing security measures, including measures to address vulnerabilities introduced by third-party data transfer via APIs.
    • Suggestions for future research directions, focusing on improving cloud security practices for CRM data and addressing emerging threats effectively.
