Questionnaire Critique: Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity (CSEC) Questionnaire & Critique

The Cybersecurity (CSEC) Questionnaire is a tool developed to assess individual differences in unintentional insider threat behaviours related to cybersecurity practices. Created by Schoenherr and Thomson in 2021, the questionnaire aims to identify basic vulnerabilities in users' practices, including updating software, changing passwords, and disclosure of information on social media platforms.

Overview:

The CSEC Questionnaire consists of six main sections:

  • Demographics: Collects information about respondents' age, computer usage in their jobs, and professional background in information sciences.
  • Cybersecurity Training: Assesses the amount of cybersecurity training respondents have received.
  • Likert Scale: Uses a scale of 1 to 5 to measure respondents' agreement with statements related to cybersecurity practices, including internet usage, application updates, password management, and social media usage.

Usage:

The questionnaire is designed to be administered to individuals to gather data on their cybersecurity behaviour and attitudes. It can be used in research studies, organisational assessments, and personnel evaluations to identify individuals who may need additional education and training in cybersecurity practices.

Development and Validation:

The CSEC Questionnaire was developed based on existing scales assessing individual differences, such as conscientiousness, extraversion, and openness. It has been validated through empirical research to demonstrate its reliability, validity, and relevance in assessing cybersecurity behaviours.

Conclusion:

Overall, the CSEC Questionnaire serves as a valuable tool for assessing individual cybersecurity practices and identifying potential vulnerabilities that may contribute to unintentional insider threats. Its simplicity, relevance, and effectiveness make it a useful instrument in various contexts, including research, organisational assessments, and personnel evaluations.

The Questionnaire and Critique

Questions:

  1. How old are you?
    • 16-19
    • 20-23
    • 24-27
    • 28-35
    • 36-43
    • 44-51
    • 52 or older
  2. How often do you use a computer in your job?
    • Never
    • Rarely
    • Often
    • Always
  3. Are you a practitioner in information sciences (e.g., network administrator)?
    • Yes
    • No
  4. Are you an educator in information sciences (e.g., a professor, instructor)?
    • Yes
    • No
  5. How many hours of cybersecurity training do you have? This can include any activities you have done in your personal or professional life.
    • Less than 30 min
    • Up to 1 hour
    • 1 to 2 hours
    • 2 to 4 hours
    • 4 to 6 hours
    • More than 6 hours
  6. On a scale of 1 (strongly agree) to 5 (strongly disagree), please rate the extent to which you agree with the following statements (Cybersecurity items are identified with an asterisk):
    • I frequently use the internet on my laptop or personal computer.
    • I frequently use the internet on my mobile phone.
    • I frequently update the applications on my laptop or personal computer.*
    • I frequently update the applications on my mobile phone.*
    • I do not change my password often. (R)*
    • I do not think about the security of my personal information. (R)*
    • I use social media often.*

Critique

  1. Clarity and Simplicity:
    • The questions are generally clear and easy to understand, with straightforward response options.
  2. Relevance:
    • The questions appear relevant to the study's objectives, focusing on individual characteristics, cybersecurity practices, and demographics.
  3. Completeness:
    • The questionnaire covers a range of relevant aspects, including age, computer usage, profession, cybersecurity training, and attitudes towards cybersecurity practices.
    • However, consider including open-ended questions: In addition to closed-ended questions, consider including some open-ended questions to allow respondents to provide more detailed insights or explanations, especially regarding their cybersecurity practices and attitudes.
  4. Balance:
    • The questions seem balanced in terms of positive and negative phrasing, with a mix of positively and negatively worded statements in the Likert scale section.
  5. Response Options:
    • The response options are appropriate and cover a range of possibilities for each question.
    • However, there could be a risk of response bias due to the lack of a neutral option in the Likert scale section.
  6. Format:
    • The format is consistent throughout the questionnaire, making it easy to follow.
    • The Likert scale questions could benefit from numbering to improve clarity and organisation.
  7. Length:
    • The length of the questionnaire seems reasonable, neither too long nor too short, considering the complexity of the topic.
  8. Pilot Testing:
    • There's no mention of pilot testing in the provided information, but it would be beneficial to pilot test the questionnaire to ensure clarity, relevance, and comprehensibility.

Overall, the questionnaire appears to be a solid tool for gathering data on cybersecurity behaviour and individual characteristics. However, some minor adjustments, such as adding numbering to Likert scale questions, could improve its effectiveness. Additionally, pilot testing would help identify any potential issues before full-scale implementation.
